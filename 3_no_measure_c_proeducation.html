How long families pay before the library opens — if it opens at all.
Tax collection begins December 2026. The most optimistic opening date is 2032. That is six years of payments for a building that doesn't exist yet.

Every dollar in parcel taxes is a dollar not spent on education.
Measure C starts at $289 per year for a typical home and rises every year. That is money El Cerrito families can no longer direct toward tutoring, books, after-school enrichment, music lessons, or school fundraisers. The tax is mandatory and escalating. The educational benefit is uncertain and years away.

The library cannot help students who have already graduated.
The new library will not open until 2032 at the earliest — and only if a competitive state grant is awarded to a developer who has never applied for that grant on this site. Any student currently in 5th grade or above will be in high school or beyond before the library opens. The children who need it most right now will be gone.
The current library is open now — and serves students today
The existing El Cerrito Library is open 46 hours per week. After school, it is already packed with children doing homework. A No vote does not close the current library. It rejects a 30-year financial commitment to a construction project that may not be built — while students need resources today.
3
El Cerrito's stalled housing pipeline threatens school funding.
California school funding follows enrollment. Enrollment follows families. Families follow housing. El Cerrito has permitted only about 7% of its state-mandated housing obligation through 2024 — one of the lowest rates in the county. The TOD project that the library depends on has itself stalled. Failing to build housing means fewer families, declining enrollment, and less funding for El Cerrito schools.
El Cerrito's 6th Cycle state housing mandate: 1,391 units by 2031. Units permitted through 2024: roughly 98 — about 7% of the obligation. The library site (Parcel C West) cannot be built until the developer wins a grant it has never applied for. Meanwhile, the housing pipeline that determines school enrollment remains nearly at a standstill.
4
A failed project wastes money that could have funded education.
Independent analysis shows the tax revenue is insufficient to cover bond payments and operating costs at investment-grade borrowing rates. The ballot measure itself caps the debt service ratio at a level that guarantees junk bond financing. A project that fails mid-construction leaves El Cerrito with a tax bill, no library, and no educational benefit — for 30 years.
