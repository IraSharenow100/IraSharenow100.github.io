1
The tax rises every year. There is no cap on how high it goes.
Measure C escalates annually by whichever is greater: Bay Area CPI or California per-capita income growth. There is no dollar ceiling. On a 1,700 sq ft home, Year 1 is $289. By Year 10 it is over $411. By Year 30 it exceeds $900. Over the life of the tax, a typical homeowner pays more than $16,000 — for a library that may not open until 2032, if ever.

2
Measure C is not the only new tax on this ballot.
El Cerrito homeowners face a stack of new taxes across both the June and November 2026 ballots — a healthcare sales tax, a regional transit measure covering BART and buses, a community college bond, a school district measure, and Measure C. The cumulative burden matters — even if each measure looks manageable in isolation.

3
You pay for years — with no guarantee a library gets built.
Construction cannot begin until a private developer wins a competitive state housing grant for a site it has never applied for. The same developer applied for a nearby parcel and was rejected in the most recent round. Tax collection starts December 2026. The earliest realistic opening is 2032. If the grant is denied, your taxes are collected and the library is not built — no refund, no recourse.
After Year 10, the risk shifts to the General Fund — and to you
The parcel tax covers library operating costs for only the first 10 years after opening. After that, the city's General Fund absorbs up to $797,000 per year in operating costs — on top of the bond payments still being collected from homeowners. The tax bill doesn't shrink after Year 10. The city's obligations grow.
