The "senior exemption" exists on paper. Not in practice.
To qualify, a senior must be approved for the California State Property Tax Postponement Program — and meet every one of its strict requirements simultaneously. The table below shows what that actually requires. Compare it to WCCUSD's Measure T, which simply required being age 65 or older — no income test, no equity test, no state program enrollment.

2
The tax rises every year. Your income doesn't.
Measure C escalates annually by whichever is greater: Bay Area CPI or California per-capita income growth. That escalation consistently outpaces Social Security cost-of-living adjustments. A tax that starts at $289 for a typical home grows to over $411 by Year 10 and over $900 by Year 30. On a fixed income, that gap only widens.

3
Tax collection starts December 2026. The library opens in 2032 at the earliest.
Construction cannot begin until a private developer wins a competitive state grant it has never applied for on this site. The same developer applied for a nearby parcel and was rejected in the most recent round. Many seniors paying this tax from 2026 onward will wait years before the library opens — if it opens at all. There is no refund if the project fails.
Six years of payments before the library opens
At $289 in Year 1, rising each year, a typical senior homeowner will pay roughly $1,800 before the library doors open in 2032. That assumes everything goes perfectly — the grant is awarded, construction starts on schedule, and there are no further delays. None of those outcomes are guaranteed.
