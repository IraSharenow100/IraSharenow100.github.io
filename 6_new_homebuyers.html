Your mortgage builds equity. This tax builds nothing for you.
If you bought recently, you're already carrying a large mortgage at elevated interest rates. Measure C adds a second 30-year financial obligation on top of it — one that escalates every year, gives you no ownership stake, and cannot be recovered if the library is never built.

The parcel tax runs 30 years after bonds are first issued — potentially 36+ years from today if construction is delayed. The City Council sets the annual rate by resolution. No further voter approval required.
2
New buyers pay full assessed value. Long-timers pay a fraction.
Under Prop 13, longtime El Cerrito owners pay property taxes on assessments that may be decades old — far below current market value. You bought at today's prices and pay today's full assessed value. The parcel tax charges everyone the same rate per square foot, but your overall property tax burden as a new buyer is already far heavier than your neighbors' who bought 20 years ago. Measure C adds one more layer to that imbalance.
The Yes campaign says the rate is the same for new and existing buyers
That's technically true for the parcel tax rate itself. But it ignores the full picture. A new buyer paying $14,000+/year in base property taxes on a $1.2M assessed home faces a very different burden than a neighbor assessed at $300,000 from a 1995 purchase. Same parcel tax rate. Very different total tax load.
3
The tax starts December 2026. The library may never be built.
Construction depends on a competitive state housing grant the developer has never applied for on this site. The same developer applied for a nearby parcel and was rejected in the most recent round. If the grant fails, your taxes are collected — and the library is not built. There is no refund. There is no recourse. The tax clock keeps running for 30 years from the date bonds are first issued, whenever that is.
The 2025 petition cited a $21.2 million construction cost. By January 2026 the city's own consultant put it at $37.2 million. Griffin Structures projects costs rising 6% per year from that baseline. Every year of delay — waiting for a grant that may never come — adds roughly $2 million to the bill your taxes are expected to cover.
4
The City Council raises the rate every year. You get no vote.
The measure authorizes the City Council to increase the maximum tax rate annually by resolution — no voter approval needed. The escalation index is whichever is greater: Bay Area CPI or California per-capita income growth. You voted once in June 2026. After that, the rate goes up automatically, every year, for the next 30+ years.
"We stretched to buy our home in El Cerrito at 7% interest. Now we're being asked to sign up for another 30-year obligation that escalates every year — for a library that won't open until 2032 at the earliest, if it opens at all. We didn't budget for this. Nobody told us it was coming."
One 30-year commitment was enough.
