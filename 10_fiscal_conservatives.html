1
The ballot measure guarantees junk bond financing.
Section 4.58.040(C) caps the tax rate at 115% of what is needed to cover bond payments and operating costs. That means the debt service coverage ratio (DSCR) cannot exceed 1.15x — by design. Investment-grade municipal bonds require a minimum DSCR of 1.25x. Junk-rated bonds carry higher interest rates, increasing the total cost to taxpayers over the life of the debt.

2
After Year 10, the General Fund absorbs up to $797,000 per year.
The parcel tax covers library operating costs for only the first ten years after opening. After that, the city's General Fund assumes full responsibility for operating and maintenance costs — estimated at $187,755 to $797,000 annually, escalating each year. The bond payments continue to be collected from taxpayers regardless. This is a structural obligation with no sunset.

3
The cost jumped 75% in nine months. No explanation was provided.
The 2025 petition cited a $21.2 million construction cost. By January 2026, the city's own consultant put it at $37.2 million. That is a $16 million increase before design began, before ground was broken, and before any contracts were signed. Griffin Structures projects further increases of 6% per year for every year construction is delayed. The City Council can raise the tax rate annually by resolution — no voter approval required.
Tax collection begins before there is any certainty of construction
The parcel tax starts in December 2026. Construction cannot begin until a competitive state housing grant is awarded — one the developer has never applied for on this site. Taxpayers begin paying immediately. The city is under no obligation to return collected funds if the grant is denied and the library is never built. There is no escrow. There is no refund mechanism. There is no sunset triggered by project failure.
