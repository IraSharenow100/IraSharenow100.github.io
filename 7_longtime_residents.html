You've heard these promises before. The record matters.
El Cerrito has been working toward a new library for more than two decades. Each iteration has come with new promises, new cost estimates, and new reasons why this time it will happen. The timeline below shows what actually occurred.
2002–2014
City explores sites, updates needs assessments, begins planning. No library built.
2015
City poll: 72% support for a new library bond. Optimism is high.
2016
Measure B placed on ballot — city hides the tax cost from voters. Financial Advisory Board warns council to include tax language. Council ignores it. Measure gets 63% yes — just short of the 66.7% required. Fails by ~500 votes.
2017–2024
Years of planning, surveys, community meetings, TOD negotiations. No library built. Cost estimates rise.
2025
Citizen petition cites $21.2 million construction cost. Nine months later, Griffin Structures puts it at $37.2 million. A 75% increase before design began.
2026
Measure C on the June ballot. Construction depends on a grant the developer has never applied for. Most optimistic opening: 2032.
2
The cost jumped 75% in nine months — before construction began.
When El Cerrito first put a library measure before voters in 2016, the price tag was $30 million. By the time the 2025 petition circulated, it was $21.2 million for the TOD version. By January 2026 it was $37.2 million — and Griffin Structures projects it rising 6% per year from there. Every year of delay adds roughly $2 million.

The city has a documented history of hiding costs from voters.
In 2014, city officials deliberately omitted that a sales tax measure would double the rate to one of the highest in California. In 2016, the Financial Advisory Board voted to require the library bond language to disclose the tax consequences. The City Council ignored that recommendation. The East Bay Times editorial board called it "one of the worst examples" of local government manipulating voters. That same culture produced Measure C.
This version is less certain than the 2016 one that failed
The 2016 Measure B was a straightforward bond — the city would borrow money and build a library. It failed, but at least the path was clear. Measure C cannot proceed until a private developer wins a competitive state grant it has never applied for on this site. The 2016 plan was more direct. This one adds a layer of uncertainty that didn't exist before — and costs significantly more.
"I've lived in El Cerrito for 30 years. I voted Yes in 2016 because I believed the numbers. Now the cost has jumped 75% in nine months and the plan depends on a grant we've never applied for. I've been patient. This plan doesn't deserve my trust."
24 years of waiting deserves an honest plan.
