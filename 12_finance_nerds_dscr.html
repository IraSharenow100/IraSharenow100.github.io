Even using the city's unverified maximum revenue of $3,156,019, the debt-only DSCR would be 1.302x — barely above 1.25x — but the ballot language cap of 1.15x legally prevents the city from setting a rate that would achieve it. The measure structurally forecloses investment-grade financing.
Section 3 — The ballot language locks in junk bond status
Section 4.58.040(C) caps the annual tax rate at 115% of the amount needed to cover debt service and operating costs. This is not a projection — it is written into the measure and cannot be changed without a new vote.
The circular logic of the city's financing model
To achieve investment-grade borrowing rates, a project needs a DSCR of at least 1.25x. The ballot language caps the DSCR at 1.15x. The city cannot achieve investment-grade status under its own ballot language. Higher borrowing costs on sub-investment-grade bonds increase total interest paid — which worsens the already-failing DSCR further. The city's 5% assumption presupposes a credit quality the project's own structure prevents it from achieving.
Section 4 — The Year 10 structural cliff
The parcel tax covers operating costs for the first ten years after opening only. After Year 10, the General Fund absorbs all operating and maintenance costs — while bond payments continue to be collected from taxpayers.
