1
The Yes campaign says renters don't pay. Here's the full story.
It's true that the parcel tax is billed to property owners, not tenants. But landlords operate businesses. When their costs rise, they look to recover those costs — through rent increases, reduced maintenance, or both. A tax that escalates every year for 30+ years is a permanent upward pressure on every rental unit in El Cerrito.

What it means for renters
"A special parcel tax is imposed on the property owner. Renters are not charged."
The tax bill goes to your landlord. Landlords pass rising costs to tenants. A tax that grows every year for 30+ years is a permanent cost increase on every rental property in El Cerrito.
2
The library opens in 2032 — if it opens at all.
Tax collection starts December 2026. The library cannot open until a competitive state housing grant is awarded to a developer who has never applied for that grant on this site. The same developer applied for a nearby parcel and was rejected in the most recent round. The most optimistic opening date is 2032 — six years from now. Many of today's El Cerrito renters will have moved before the library ever opens.
Renters absorb the cost — without the ownership benefit
Homeowners who pay this tax own an asset that may appreciate over time. Renters get no such benefit. If your landlord raises your rent to offset the tax, you pay — with nothing to show for it if the library is never built. There is no refund mechanism if the project fails.
3
The library blocks the affordable housing El Cerrito actually needs.
The library is planned for the ground floor of a housing building at the El Cerrito Plaza BART station. That housing project depends on the same state grant the library does — one the developer has never applied for here. As long as the library plan is tied to this uncertain grant, the affordable housing units planned for that site are also at risk. El Cerrito has permitted only about 7% of its state-mandated housing obligation. Renters need more housing — not another reason to delay it.
The TOD project at El Cerrito Plaza includes affordable rental units for low-income families. Those units depend on the same AHSC state grant the library depends on. The developer's most recent application — for a nearby parcel — was rejected in Round 9 (2024). Parcel C West, the library site, has never even applied. Fewer affordable units means a tighter rental market for everyone.
4
The tax rises every year — so does the pressure on your rent.
Measure C escalates annually by whichever is greater: Bay Area CPI or California per-capita income growth. A tax that starts at $289/year on a typical home grows to over $411 by Year 10 and over $900 by Year 30. That is three decades of rising landlord costs in a city where rents are already high.
"They told me this tax doesn't apply to renters. But my landlord's costs go up — and so does my rent. I'm paying for a library I may never see, in a city I may not be living in by 2032."
Renters deserve honest answers — not half the story.
