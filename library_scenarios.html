El Cerrito Library Tax: How Does It Affect City Finances?

A data analysis of six financial scenarios for the proposed 17¢/sq ft parcel tax on the June 2026 ballot. Each scenario states its assumptions explicitly, then follows the arithmetic wherever it leads.

Analysis: Ira Sharenow, El Cerrito  ·  Developed in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic)  ·  Sources: City §9212 Impact Report (Feb. 19, 2026); Nov. 2023 City Council presentation; Griffin Structures cost estimates

Analyst's note

I am a data analyst. My method: state the assumptions explicitly, then follow the math. The six scenarios below range from the city's original 2023 baseline to a reverse calculation of what this tax can actually afford to build. We assume the project proceeds as quickly as possible — no delay penalties applied. The key question: does the money add up? In five of six scenarios, it does not. In every scenario, the General Fund absorbs a permanent and growing burden after year 10.

Bond Credit Rating & General Fund Implications

Goal: A-Rated Bond at 5%

An A rating requires a Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) of at least 1.25×. Revenue must equal 125% of debt service. However, the ballot initiative caps the tax at 115% of costs — structurally preventing an A rating from tax revenue alone.


GF subsidy needed annually to achieve A rating
~$527,000/yr
1.25× DSCR requires $3,026,844 revenue. Tax provides $2,500,000. Gap = $526,844.
If AA Bonds Were Achievable (~4.4%)

AA bonds at ~4.4% would reduce annual debt service to approximately $2,200,000 — saving about $221,000/year vs. 5% bonds, or $6.6M over 30 years. But AA requires DSCR ≥ 1.5×, which this tax cannot produce. AA is not achievable.


Realistic rating given 1.15× structural cap
Below Investment Grade

General Fund Impact: Three Phases

Phase 1 · Pre-Construction (Project Starts ASAP)
$157,615/yr (current)
GF currently pays library O&M. Tax collections begin December 2026. Funds go to planning, permitting, and administration while construction is underway. We assume the project proceeds as fast as possible.
Phase 2 · Years 1–10 After Opening
$821K+ gap, growing each year
Tax covers debt service but falls short of total costs from day one. The 3%/yr operating cost escalation widens the gap every year. By year 10, the annual shortfall reaches ~$988K.
Phase 3 · After Year 10 — Permanent GF Burden
$900K → $1.21M/yr and rising
The 10-year operating authorization expires. All operating costs — grown from $900K to over $1.21M at 3%/yr — revert permanently to the General Fund. That is a 7.7× increase over today's $157,615. No plan exists to fund this.

Bond Credit Rating — Even Best Case Has Structural Problems

Year-1 DSCR

$3,156,019 ÷ $2,421,475 = 1.30× — enough for an A rating in Year 1. But this requires maximum rate every year AND operating costs at half the city's own estimate. Any revenue shortfall or cost increase destroys the coverage ratio.

The 3%/Year Deterioration Problem

Operating costs growing at 3%/yr erode the DSCR every year. By approximately Year 5, total costs exceed revenue and the coverage ratio drops below 1.0×. Bond rating agencies would anticipate this deterioration and likely price the bonds accordingly at issuance.

The Arithmetic

Revenue available for debt service + reserve = $2,700,000 − $700,000 = $2,000,000
With 15% reserve requirement, net available for debt service = $2,000,000 ÷ 1.15 = $1,739,130
Maximum loan at 5%/30yr (PMT reverse): $1,739,130 ÷ 0.06505 ≈ $26,730,000

Affordable sq ft = ($26,730,000 ÷ $37,200,000) × 20,000 = ~14,370 sq ft

The proposed library is ~5,630 sq ft too large for what this tax revenue can support — roughly 28% oversized.

Risks That Could Make the Affordable Size Even Smaller

Interest Rate Risk
+1% rate → −1,150 sq ft
If bond rate is 6% instead of 5%, annual debt service on $26.7M rises from $1,739K to $1,921K — exceeding what's available. The maximum affordable loan drops to ~$23.4M, reducing the affordable library size to ~12,600 sq ft.
Revenue Risk
−10% revenue → −1,400 sq ft
If actual revenue comes in 10% below mid-range ($2,430,000 instead of $2,700,000), only $1,730,000 remains after operations — reducing the maximum affordable loan by ~$2.6M and the affordable library to ~12,970 sq ft.
Operating Cost Risk
+3%/yr compounding narrows capacity every year
By year 5, operating costs at 3%/yr grow from $700K to $811K — reducing the amount available for debt service. The "affordable" calculation shown here uses Year 1 figures only. The financial capacity shrinks every year the library stays open.

General Fund Impact: Three Phases — Even for an Affordable Library

Phase 1 · Pre-Construction
$157,615/yr (current)
Tax collected before construction. A smaller library still requires planning, permitting, and construction — with all the same risks and timeline challenges.
Phase 2 · Years 1–10 After Opening
Near break-even at start
A properly sized ~14,000 sq ft library breaks even in year 1 under mid-range assumptions. But the 3%/yr operating escalation begins eroding the margin immediately — requiring GF support by year 5 or 6.
Phase 3 · After Year 10 — Permanent GF Burden
$700K → $940K/yr and rising
Even a smaller, properly sized library imposes the same Phase 3 burden. Operating costs grown from $700K to ~$940K hit the GF permanently at year 10 — regardless of library size. This burden does not go away.
Bottom line: The tax revenue cannot support the proposed 20,000 sq ft library at mid-range assumptions. To build what is proposed, the city must either scale back to ~14,000 sq ft, find $10.5M in outside funding not yet identified, or ask voters to approve a higher rate than 17¢. None of these options has been presented to voters. And in every scenario — regardless of library size — operating costs become a permanent General Fund obligation after year 10.
