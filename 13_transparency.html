4 times
El Cerrito has concealed, withheld, or refused to disclose information about library costs and financing — across 2014, 2016, 2023, and 2026.
This is not a pattern of mistakes. It is a pattern of choices.
The Yes campaign promotes "independent citizen oversight" and "mandatory financial audits" as accountability safeguards. But voters should evaluate those promises in light of what the city has actually done — not what it says it will do.
2014
City deliberately hid that a ballot measure would double the sales tax
Documents and emails obtained through the state Public Records Act revealed that city officials deliberately omitted from the 75-word ballot language that Measure R would double El Cerrito's sales tax to one of the highest rates in California. The city's own financial advisor confirmed the omission. The East Bay Times called it "a deceptive campaign."
2016
Financial Advisory Board warned council — council ignored it
The city's own Financial Advisory Board voted 3–1 to require the library bond ballot language to disclose the tax consequences to voters. The City Council ignored the recommendation. The ballot language for Measure B contained no mention that it was a tax. FAB chair Rich Bartke said: "We wanted to make it more open and transparent about what's going on, but the council didn't adopt any word change." Measure B failed — but the conduct did not change.
2023
Three Public Records Act requests denied before a key Council presentation
Before the November 21, 2023 Council presentation, a resident requested three taxpayer-funded reports. All three were denied. The October 2023 survey results — cited in city presentations but never released — remain undisclosed to this day. The city told voters what the surveys showed when the numbers were favorable; it withheld them when they were not.
2026
City refused to release data supporting its own revenue projection
The §9212 Impact Report claims annual maximum tax revenue of $3,156,019 based on 18,458,937 taxable square feet. A Public Records Act request for the parcel-level data supporting that figure was denied. Independent analysis using the WCCUSD Measure T roll — the best available public proxy — yields $2,699,075. The $457,000 gap cannot be verified because the city refuses to show its work.

The cost jumped 75% in nine months — no explanation was given
The 2025 petition cited $21.2 million. The January 2026 Griffin Structures estimate is $37.2 million. That is a $16 million increase before design began, before contracts were signed, and before ground was broken. No public explanation was provided for why the cost increased so dramatically in under a year. Voters are being asked to approve a 30-year tax based on numbers that changed by 75% with no accounting.
The city's own 2023 polling — which it paid for and then withheld — showed that the statement "the money will not be spent as promised" was the single most persuasive concern among likely voters, scoring 0.97 out of 2.0. The city knew voters distrusted how the money would be spent. It suppressed the full survey results and put the measure on the ballot anyway.
"I asked for the financial analysis. Denied. I asked for the survey results. Denied. I asked for the revenue calculation. Denied. They want me to trust them with 30 years of my tax dollars while refusing to show me their work."
Transparency is not a promise. It's a record.
